Diwali

Day 1: Dhanteras

  • Marks the beginning of Diwali festivities.
  • People clean homes and buy new utensils, gold, or silver as a symbol of prosperity.
  • Small pujas for wealth and well-being are performed.
  • Homes are decorated with rangoli and diyas.

Day 2: Naraka Chaturdashi / Choti Diwali

  • Celebrates the victory of Lord Krishna over the demon Narakasura.
  • People wake up early, take a ritual bath with oils and herbs, and wear new clothes.
  • Small-scale celebrations and lighting diyas begin in homes.
  • Some regions also observe Lakshmi Puja preparations.

Day 3: Lakshmi Puja / Main Diwali

  • The most important day of Diwali.
    • Families perform Lakshmi Puja in the evening:
    • Worship Goddess Lakshmi (wealth) and Lord Ganesha (remover of obstacles).
    • Offer sweets, fruits, flowers, and money to the deities.
  • Homes are fully decorated with lights, diyas, and rangoli.
  • Fireworks and crackers are set off after the puja.
  • Families exchange gifts and sweets with relatives and neighbors.

Day 4: Govardhan Puja / Annakut

  • Celebrates Lord Krishna lifting Govardhan Hill to protect villagers from floods.
  • People prepare a large variety of vegetarian food to offer to Krishna.
  • Homes and temples display miniature Govardhan Hill setups with food items (called Annakut).

Day 5: Bhai Dooj

  • Shopping and fairs often happen throughout these days.
  • New clothes and jewelry are worn each day.
  • Lighting and decorations gradually increase until the main Diwali night.
  • Modern celebrations also focus on eco-friendly practices, reducing excessive firecracker use.
